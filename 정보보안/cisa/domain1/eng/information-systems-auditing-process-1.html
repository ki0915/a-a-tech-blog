CISA Audit Documentation and Risk Management

1. CISA Audit Documentation Types

IS Audit Charter

A document that comprehensively defines the roles and responsibilities of the audit function. It includes details about responsibilities, authority, and duties and must be approved by the board or sponsor.

IS Audit Program

A document that records the process of planning and executing the audit. Approval must be obtained before the audit begins, and it can be urgently modified only in significant cases. It details the procedures for on-site audits.

IS Audit Work Papers

Documents that record all audit procedures and techniques used during the audit. Electronic audit work papers should have well-configured access permissions. Work papers must be documented for quality assurance, reference by other auditors, or litigation purposes.

2. Follow-Up Review

Examine whether the management's follow-up review, based on the findings and recommendations presented by the IS auditor, was adequate.

3. Audit Evidence

Audit evidence is evaluated based on the following criteria:

  • Relevance: How related the evidence is to the audit.
  • Reliability:
    • Where it was obtained (from independent third parties rather than internal sources is more reliable).
    • Who collected it (whether it was collected by an authoritative body, e.g., international auditing institutions).
    • How objective it is.
    • When it was collected (how much time has passed since the event).
  • Usefulness: Whether the evidence is helpful to the audit.
  • Sufficiency: Whether the evidence is sufficient to lead to the same conclusions by anyone reviewing it.

4. Types of Risk

Here are the types of risks frequently examined:

Inherent Risk

Threats that inherently exist and can be reduced through compensating controls, but are nearly impossible for auditors to control.

Control Risk

Threats arising when errors are not detected and corrected. While short-term improvements may be limited, long-term improvements are possible based on the auditor’s recommendations.

Detection Risk

Divided into sampling risk and non-sampling risk:

  • Sampling Risk: The risk that the sample selected does not represent the population.
  • Non-Sampling Risk: Problems arising during audit procedures due to auditor mistakes or deficiencies. This risk can be mitigated through training, education, and adjusting the audit's intensity.

Audit Risk

The risk that the auditor issues a report without detecting significant issues. Audit risk is calculated as the product of inherent risk, control risk, and detection risk.

Business Risk

The risk that management is willing to accept. Auditors must ensure that audit risk is less than business risk.

5. Risk Management

Effective risk management involves a thorough understanding of the organization's risk propensity. Which document best captures the organization's risk propensity?

Risk Management Strategy Sequence

  1. Define Risk Propensity: For example, accounting-related issues.
  2. Identify Risks: For instance, exposure of accounting records.
  3. Develop Risk Management Strategy: Based on the above information.

Five Risk Control Measures

  • Avoidance (Eliminating the Cause): Completely removes the cause of the risk, thus eliminating the risk. This is the most certain method but is costly.
  • Mitigation (Reducing the Risk): Implement internal measures to reduce risk. For example, if API security is weak, use VPN to restrict access to authorized users only.
  • Transfer: Use third parties to handle the risk or transfer responsibility. Examples include insurance and outsourcing.
  • Acceptance (Acknowledging the Risk): Knowing about the risk but taking no action. Auditors should check if accepting the risk is appropriate.
  • Ignoring: Not acknowledging or responding to the risk.

6. Risk Management Process

  1. Risk Identification: Identify assets.
  2. Risk Analysis/Evaluation: Analyze and evaluate risks.
  3. Risk Control: Apply controls. Evaluate and confirm existing controls at this stage.
  4. Ongoing Monitoring: After applying controls, residual risk remains. Auditors recommend measures to mitigate this risk. Management decides the acceptable level of risk.
  5. Periodic Reporting: Regular reporting is necessary.

7. Risk Analysis

Risk Analysis Frequency

  1. When significant changes or threats occur (Priority 1)
  2. At least once a year (Priority 2)

Risk Analysis Methods

Qualitative Methods

  • Delphi Method: Consulting with experts anonymously.
  • Questionnaire Method: Analysis through questionnaires.
  • Scenario Method: Analysis through various scenarios.

Quantitative Methods

  • Expressing Threat Likelihood Numerically: Quantitative approach.
  • Complex Method: Can be complex.

Risk-Based Audit (RBAA)

  • Complete elimination of all errors or fraud is not possible.
  • The principle of materiality applies, and auditors must make professional judgments.
  • Examine high-risk areas first to use resources efficiently.
