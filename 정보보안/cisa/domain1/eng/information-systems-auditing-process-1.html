CISA Audit Documentation and Risk Management

<h2>1. CISA Audit Documentation Types</h2>
<h3>IS Audit Charter</h3>
<p class="highlight">A document that comprehensively defines the roles and responsibilities of the audit function. It includes details about responsibilities, authority, and duties and must be approved by the board or sponsor.</p>

<h3>IS Audit Program</h3>
<p class="highlight">A document that records the process of planning and executing the audit. Approval must be obtained before the audit begins, and it can be urgently modified only in significant cases. It details the procedures for on-site audits.</p>

<h3>IS Audit Work Papers</h3>
<p class="highlight">Documents that record all audit procedures and techniques used during the audit. Electronic audit work papers should have well-configured access permissions. Work papers must be documented for quality assurance, reference by other auditors, or litigation purposes.</p>

<h2>2. Follow-Up Review</h2>
<p>Examine whether the management's follow-up review, based on the findings and recommendations presented by the IS auditor, was adequate.</p>

<h2>3. Audit Evidence</h2>
<p>Audit evidence is evaluated based on the following criteria:</p>
<ul>
    <li><span class="important">Relevance:</span> How related the evidence is to the audit.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Reliability:</span> 
        <ul>
            <li>Where it was obtained (from independent third parties rather than internal sources is more reliable).</li>
            <li>Who collected it (whether it was collected by an authoritative body, e.g., international auditing institutions).</li>
            <li>How objective it is.</li>
            <li>When it was collected (how much time has passed since the event).</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li><span class="important">Usefulness:</span> Whether the evidence is helpful to the audit.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Sufficiency:</span> Whether the evidence is sufficient to lead to the same conclusions by anyone reviewing it.</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. Types of Risk</h2>
<p>Here are the types of risks frequently examined:</p>

<h3>Inherent Risk</h3>
<p class="highlight">Threats that inherently exist and can be reduced through compensating controls, but are nearly impossible for auditors to control.</p>

<h3>Control Risk</h3>
<p class="highlight">Threats arising when errors are not detected and corrected. While short-term improvements may be limited, long-term improvements are possible based on the auditor’s recommendations.</p>

<h3>Detection Risk</h3>
<p class="highlight">Divided into sampling risk and non-sampling risk:</p>
<ul>
    <li><span class="important">Sampling Risk:</span> The risk that the sample selected does not represent the population.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Non-Sampling Risk:</span> Problems arising during audit procedures due to auditor mistakes or deficiencies. This risk can be mitigated through training, education, and adjusting the audit's intensity.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Audit Risk</h3>
<p class="highlight">The risk that the auditor issues a report without detecting significant issues. Audit risk is calculated as the product of inherent risk, control risk, and detection risk.</p>

<h3>Business Risk</h3>
<p class="highlight">The risk that management is willing to accept. Auditors must ensure that audit risk is less than business risk.</p>

<h2>5. Risk Management</h2>
<p>Effective risk management involves a thorough understanding of the organization's risk propensity. Which document best captures the organization's risk propensity?</p>

<h3>Risk Management Strategy Sequence</h3>
<ol>
    <li><span class="important">Define Risk Propensity:</span> For example, accounting-related issues.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Identify Risks:</span> For instance, exposure of accounting records.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Develop Risk Management Strategy:</span> Based on the above information.</li>
</ol>

<h3>Five Risk Control Measures</h3>
<ul>
    <li><span class="important">Avoidance (Eliminating the Cause):</span> Completely removes the cause of the risk, thus eliminating the risk. This is the most certain method but is costly.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Mitigation (Reducing the Risk):</span> Implement internal measures to reduce risk. For example, if API security is weak, use VPN to restrict access to authorized users only.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Transfer:</span> Use third parties to handle the risk or transfer responsibility. Examples include insurance and outsourcing.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Acceptance (Acknowledging the Risk):</span> Knowing about the risk but taking no action. Auditors should check if accepting the risk is appropriate.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Ignoring:</span> Not acknowledging or responding to the risk.</li>
</ul>

<h2>6. Risk Management Process</h2>
<ol>
    <li><span class="important">Risk Identification:</span> Identify assets.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Risk Analysis/Evaluation:</span> Analyze and evaluate risks.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Risk Control:</span> Apply controls. Evaluate and confirm existing controls at this stage.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Ongoing Monitoring:</span> After applying controls, residual risk remains. Auditors recommend measures to mitigate this risk. Management decides the acceptable level of risk.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Periodic Reporting:</span> Regular reporting is necessary.</li>
</ol>

<h2>7. Risk Analysis</h2>
<h3>Risk Analysis Frequency</h3>
<ol>
    <li><span class="important">When significant changes or threats occur (Priority 1)</span></li>
    <li><span class="important">At least once a year (Priority 2)</span></li>
</ol>

<h3>Risk Analysis Methods</h3>
<h4>Qualitative Methods</h4>
<ul>
    <li><span class="important">Delphi Method:</span> Consulting with experts anonymously.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Questionnaire Method:</span> Analysis through questionnaires.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Scenario Method:</span> Analysis through various scenarios.</li>
</ul>
<h4>Quantitative Methods</h4>
<ul>
    <li><span class="important">Expressing Threat Likelihood Numerically:</span> Quantitative approach.</li>
    <li><span class="important">Complex Method:</span> Can be complex.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Risk-Based Audit (RBAA)</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Complete elimination of all errors or fraud is not possible.</li>
    <li>The principle of materiality applies, and auditors must make professional judgments.</li>
    <li>Examine high-risk areas first to use resources efficiently.</li>
</ul>
