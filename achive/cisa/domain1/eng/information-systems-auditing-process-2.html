Field Audit

Field audit planning is broadly divided into General Controls (testing the environment and operations) and Application Controls. It is important to distinguish between these and make accurate judgments.

Control Evaluation
  • Evaluate whether the control is suitable for the current situation or environment.
General Controls (Compliance Testing)
  • Tests the organization’s environment as a whole.
  • Tests compliance with rules or the appropriateness of the rules.
Application Controls (Substantive Testing)
  • Verifies the quality of actual performance.
  • Demonstrates controls that are not well followed.
  • Verifies the integrity of actual processing.
  • Validates the results.
  • Actually performs tests (e.g., checking if the financial statements are correct).

Reporting

  • Discuss findings with the auditee. Ensure they understand and agree with the findings.
  • Conduct an exit meeting with the auditor and distribute the audit report.

Follow-Up

  • Review to determine if corrective actions by management on recommendations have achieved the intended purpose.
  • No documentation or follow-up is necessary if management has decided to accept the risk.
**What is the purpose of the IS auditor’s review of management’s corrective actions?

To determine whether the corrective actions have been successfully implemented to reduce or eliminate the risk, based on the auditor’s recommendations.

Types of Controls

These are definitely included in exams

  • Preventive Controls: Controls that prevent problems before they occur.
    • Segregation of duties (task separation, job rotation), physical access controls, transaction approval procedures, etc.
  • Detective Controls: Controls that detect and report problems.
    • Hash totals, checkpoints, audits, access logs
  • Corrective Controls: Controls that minimize threats and restore systems to normal operation.
    • BCP (Business Continuity Planning), recovery and backup, risk reduction through corrective actions.
  • Compensating Controls: Controls that supplement existing controls to address potential or existing risks.
    • Reduces inherent risks through compensating controls.
    • Example: Finding patches to address vulnerabilities in irreplaceable existing modules.

Audit Testing

Control Testing (Compliance Testing)

  • Verifies whether control procedures are followed as defined.
  • Tests compliance with rules and their effectiveness.
  • This is an environmental test.
  • Uses attribute sampling.

Substantive Testing

  • Verifies the integrity of actual processing.
  • Tests the integrity of specific processes or applications (more detailed).
  • Uses variable sampling.
  • Actual performance tests.

Audit Sampling

  • Statistical Sampling: Provides an objective measure of error rates with an objective standard.
  • Random Sampling: Sampling is done randomly.
  • Systematic Sampling: The first sample is randomly selected, and subsequent samples are selected at regular intervals.
  • Non-Statistical Sampling: Sampling is done arbitrarily.
  • Attribute Sampling: Used for compliance testing and general controls.
    • Divided into interval sampling and discovery sampling.
    • Interval Sampling: Continues sampling until the sample’s population error rate is lower than expected results, and stops when a population meeting the expected results is obtained.
    • Discovery Sampling: Sampling used to detect errors or fraud.
  • Variable Sampling: Used for substantive testing and application controls.
    • Verifies the integrity of actual results.
  • Stratified Sampling: Divides the population based on specific criteria. Reduces sample size by limiting the sampling target population.

CATTs (Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques)

  • GAS (General Audit Software): General-purpose audit software.
  • SAS (Specialized Audit Software): Software used for specific purposes.
  • White Box: Audits by looking into the internal logic.
  • Black Box: Audits by examining external functionality only.

12. CSA (Control Self-Assessment)

  • Refers to self-auditing.
  • Identifies high-risk areas.
  • The auditor acts as a facilitator.
  • Can boost the morale of internal staff.
  • Conducted in workshop format.
  • Evaluation is done by management.
  • CSA is led by operational managers.

13. IS Code of Ethics

  1. ISACA establishes this code of ethics to provide guidance on the professional and personal conduct of its members and certification holders.
  2. ISACA members and certification holders must adhere to the following:
    1. Support and promote adherence to effective governance, management, auditing, controls, security, and risk management standards and procedures applicable to information systems and technology.
    2. Perform their duties with objectivity, due diligence, and professional care according to the standards of their profession.
    3. Maintain high standards of conduct and integrity, avoid actions that may discredit their profession or association, and contribute to stakeholders’ benefits through lawful means.
    4. Maintain the privacy and confidentiality of information obtained during the performance of their duties unless required by law. Information should not be used for personal gain or disclosed to inappropriate parties.
    5. Agree to perform work only if they have the necessary skills, knowledge, and ability to complete it effectively.
    6. Disclose all significant facts that may distort the results of their work and communicate the results to appropriate parties (to the audited department, not to other departments!).
    7. Support the understanding of stakeholders about the governance, management, auditing, controls, security, and risk management of information systems and technology by promoting their professional education.

Governance

Types of Governance

  • Corporate Governance: Controls to achieve corporate objectives.
  • Information Protection Governance: Aims at protection and improving operational efficiency.
  • IT Governance: Strategic alignment, value delivery (benefit-based), performance measurement (using BSC for IT performance measurement) -> Sharing organizational strategy. Includes resource management and risk management.
    • Management recognizes risks.
    • The ideal situation is understanding the organization’s risk appetite.
    • Responsibility always lies with the organization.
  • Compliance (Policy Management)

Role of IT Governance Audits

  • Continuously monitors policies, laws, regulations, etc.
  • Reviews IT governance and identifies areas for improvement.
  • Recommends best practices to management.
  • Assists in ensuring policy compliance.
  • Evaluates with an independent and objective perspective.

Objectives: Increase resource efficiency, protect resources, implement IT strategy.

  • Strategic alignment between business and IT.
  • Effective IT governance.
    • Demonstrate leadership to ensure assurance.
    • Ensure proper organizational structure.
    • Support the effective execution of processes.

IS Operations Committee

  • Responsibilities: Prioritize projects, schedule projects, allocate project resources, mediate opinions between teams.

Information Protection Governance

  • Expected Benefits of Information Protection Governance:
    • Provides reliability to trading partners and customers.
    • Offers better efficiency.
    • Enhances corporate image.
