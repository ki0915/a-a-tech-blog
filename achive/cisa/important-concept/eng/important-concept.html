Key Points for Information Systems Operations, Maintenance, and Management

  1. Before an Audit:

    • It is crucial to input information related to "threats" (threat assessment) because audits are related to threats.

  2. Information Classification:

    • Performed to prevent data loss and ensure protection.

  3. KRI (Key Risk Indicator):

    • Detects unacceptable threat levels or changes in threats, not the discovery of new threats.

  4. Auditor's Actions:

    • If findings are discovered, it is preferable to report them to the audit committee or the subject before documenting them. For example, the audit manager.

  5. Information Security Framework:

    • Includes compliance with regulations and legal requirements. The framework is broader than policies and is crucial in developing information security policies.

  6. Development Methodologies in IT Management and Standards:

    • Must include risk management techniques to meet information security requirements.

  7. Control Summation:

    • Ensures "processing integrity."

  8. Small Organizations:

    • When role separation is difficult, monitoring is usually the most appropriate option compared to review.

  9. BCP (Business Continuity Planning):

    • Testing is more critical than having updates; a lack of testing is a more significant issue.

  10. Escrow Agreements:

    • Performed to ensure continuity. Therefore, escrow agreements should always be implemented to guarantee continuity. For example, an escrow repository must always have the latest version of the code.

  11. Certificate Lifespan:

    • Managed by the Certificate Authority (CA). The Certificate Revocation List (CRL) only provides a list of revoked certificates.

  12. Checksum:

    • Is a "preventive" control.

  13. Information Security Policy:

    • Should consider the risk management process. Although considering the information security framework is also a good approach, it may not account for specific organizational circumstances.

  14. Balanced Scorecard (BSC):

    • Measures the efficiency of IT governance.

  15. Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB):

    • Provides visibility and control over data in the cloud.

  16. Program Changes:

    • Must be "thoroughly tested" to ensure integrity, confidentiality, and compliance with requirements.

  17. Data Classification:

    • The first step in data protection.

  18. Configuration Management System:

    • One of its purposes is to define and manage "software baselines."

  19. Enterprise Risk Management (ERM):

    • The responsibility of the Board of Directors.

  20. Tokenization and Masking:

    • Tokenization: Replaces sensitive data with randomly generated tokens, storing the tokens in the database and keeping the actual data elsewhere for confidentiality.
    • Masking: Obscures some or all parts of sensitive data. It maintains confidentiality while allowing users to recognize their own identification information.

  21. SIEM (Security Information and Event Management):

    • Collects and consolidates various logs and events into a single source of information.

  22. Information Security Policies:

    • Should consider the risk management process.

  23. Automated Internal Controls:

    • Capable of reviewing large-scale data.

  24. Help Desk:

    • The most critical aspect is the customer's evaluation since it is used by customers frequently.

  25. Agile Development Methodology:

    • Requires rigorous release management due to rapid changes and deployments.

  26. Auditing Projects:

    • When planning or selecting an audit project, it is important to consider the project's benefits and justification. Therefore, reviewing the business case of the project is crucial for audit project selection.

  27. Asset Disposal Process:

    • The most important aspect is the data stored on the asset, followed by ensuring the process securely disposes of the asset.

  28. Audit Committee:

    • Best suited for reporting findings and disputes due to its independence from operations.

  29. IT Governance:

    • The starting point for ensuring responsibilities are met is making the information widely known across the organization.

  30. DLP (Data Loss Prevention):

    • Training on information assets and security alongside DLP significantly contributes to preventing data leaks.

  31. Portfolio Management:

    • Aligns projects with organizational requirements and strategy. If this alignment is lacking, projects may not meet organizational needs and remain unused.

  32. DLP Systems:

    • Should detect and monitor malicious traffic.

  33. Disk Destruction:

    • If a disk is to be reused and cannot be destroyed, it should be overwritten multiple times.

  34. Incident Management Reporting:

    • Should be independent.

  35. Thin Clients:

    • Reduce the need for management.

  36. Most Comprehensive Information on Risk:

    • Provided by risk assessment.

  37. Evidence:

    • Should be extracted from "copies" rather than originals.

  38. Visitor Procedures:

    • For data centers or servers, the best procedure is for employees to escort the visitors.

  39. Outsourcing Critical Processes:

    • Requires audit rights to prevent significant issues like fraud. Continuous monitoring is essential.

  40. Incident Response Capabilities:

    • The most important aspect is documenting lessons learned to avoid repeating the same incidents.

  41. Using Risk Assessment in Audits:

    • The main reason is to allocate resources efficiently to the audit area.

  42. Disaster Situations:

    • For data center or server backups, ensure that backups are stored "off-site" due to potential complete destruction of the data center or server building.

  43. Outsourcing:

    • The main advantage is accessing skilled personnel and services at a lower cost.

  44. Virtual Infrastructure Risks:

    • Standardization of images is crucial to avoid major risks.

  45. Checksum:

    • Ensures integrity, i.e., the correctness of data.
